# Scam Warning Signs

## Document Purpose

### Metadata Labels
- doc_type: guidance_note
- audience: users, members, support, AI
- priority: high
- canonical_voice: first_person_plural

This document explains the warning signs that can indicate fraudulent opportunities. It is intended to help Bintu answer user questions about scam awareness, verification, and safer decision-making across jobs, grants, training programmes, and other opportunity pathways.

This guidance should help users protect their time, money, and personal information.

## Introduction

### Metadata Labels
- topic: introduction
- audience: all
- priority: high

In today's opportunity environment, the search for jobs, grants, training programmes, and financial advancement increasingly happens through digital platforms.

While digital access has improved convenience and reach, it has also created conditions in which fraudulent actors can exploit urgency, hope, and limited information.

Recognising scam warning signs is therefore an essential life and career skill. This guidance note exists to help users make safer decisions and avoid preventable harm.

## 1. Offers That Appear Unrealistically Attractive

### Metadata Labels
- topic: unrealistic_offers
- audience: all
- priority: high

One of the most common indicators of fraud is an offer that promises unusually high rewards with very little effort or qualification.

Users should question opportunities that offer:
- Very high salaries for entry-level work
- Instant job confirmation without assessment
- Guaranteed grants or funding without clear criteria
- Remote roles with vague responsibilities but strong financial incentives

Legitimate organisations usually provide more balanced descriptions of both benefits and expectations.

## 2. Requests For Upfront Payments

### Metadata Labels
- topic: upfront_payments
- audience: all
- priority: high

A major warning sign is any request for payment as a condition for accessing an opportunity.

This may appear as:
- Application processing fees
- Training charges
- Onboarding charges
- Security deposits
- Facilitation payments intended to speed up selection

In most credible recruitment and grant systems, applicants are not expected to pay in advance. Any financial request should be treated carefully and verified independently before action is taken.

## 3. Pressure To Act Immediately

### Metadata Labels
- topic: urgency
- audience: all
- priority: high

Fraudulent schemes often rely on urgency to prevent careful thinking.

Users should be cautious when:
- Deadlines appear artificially short
- They are told the opportunity will disappear within hours
- They are discouraged from discussing the offer with others
- They are repeatedly contacted to force quick decisions

Professional organisations generally allow reasonable time for review, questions, and preparation.

## 4. Unprofessional Communication Patterns

### Metadata Labels
- topic: communication_red_flags
- audience: all
- priority: high

The tone and structure of communication can reveal important trust signals.

Potential red flags include:
- Frequent spelling or grammatical errors
- Inconsistent organisation names
- Inconsistent contact details
- Generic greetings instead of more structured communication
- Requests handled only through informal messaging platforms without official documentation

Clear and structured communication is more typical of credible institutions.

## 5. Lack Of Verifiable Organisational Information

### Metadata Labels
- topic: organisation_verification
- audience: all
- priority: high

Before engaging seriously with any opportunity, users should confirm the legitimacy of the organisation behind it.

Warning signs can include:
- No official website
- Little or no digital presence
- Recently created social media accounts with limited activity
- No physical address
- No traceable leadership information
- Difficulty finding independent references to the organisation

Verification across multiple sources significantly reduces exposure to risk.

## 6. Requests For Sensitive Personal Information Too Early

### Metadata Labels
- topic: sensitive_information
- audience: all
- priority: high

Some personal data may be necessary during formal recruitment or onboarding stages, but early or excessive requests can indicate fraud.

Users should exercise caution if asked to provide:
- Bank account details before any formal offer
- Identity documents without a clear explanation of purpose
- Passwords
- Confidential verification codes
- Personal contacts for unexplained reasons

Responsible organisations usually request sensitive information only after formal selection or clearly explained administrative stages.

## 7. Poorly Defined Role Descriptions

### Metadata Labels
- topic: vague_roles
- audience: all
- priority: medium

Fraudulent opportunities often use vague language to avoid accountability.

Users should look for clarity regarding:
- Specific duties
- Performance expectations
- Reporting structures
- Work location
- Operational processes
- Evaluation criteria

Ambiguity can hide unrealistic, exploitative, or misleading arrangements.

## 8. Unusual Payment Or Communication Channels

### Metadata Labels
- topic: unofficial_channels
- audience: all
- priority: high

Requests to transact or communicate through unusual channels can signal risk.

Examples include:
- Payments requested through personal accounts instead of institutional channels
- Instructions to communicate only through private messaging apps
- Formal correspondence sent from unofficial email domains

Transparent financial and communication systems are common indicators of legitimate organisations.

## 9. Testimonials Or Success Stories That Cannot Be Verified

### Metadata Labels
- topic: unverifiable_testimonials
- audience: all
- priority: medium

Some fraudulent schemes try to build trust using fabricated testimonials or exaggerated success stories.

Users should:
- Verify named individuals independently where possible
- Check whether the same testimonial appears repeatedly across unrelated platforms
- Assess whether the claimed outcomes follow realistic timelines and processes

Evidence-based credibility is more reliable than emotional persuasion.

## 10. Emotional Manipulation Tactics

### Metadata Labels
- topic: emotional_manipulation
- audience: all
- priority: medium

Fraudulent actors may appeal strongly to fear, desperation, or excitement in order to override careful judgement.

Users should stay alert when:
- Messages emphasize once-in-a-lifetime chances
- They are told that many others are already benefiting without proof
- They feel pushed to act against their normal judgement

Maintaining calm evaluation improves decision quality and lowers risk.

## 11. Steps To Take When You Suspect A Scam

### Metadata Labels
- topic: protective_actions
- audience: all
- priority: high

If an opportunity raises concern, practical protective steps include:
- Pause communication until verification is completed
- Consult trusted mentors, platforms, or institutions
- Search for independent reviews or scam warnings
- Avoid financial transactions until legitimacy is confirmed
- Report suspicious activity where possible

Proactive caution helps prevent avoidable loss.
